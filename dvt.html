What Is Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)?

Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is a blood clot that forms in a deep vein, most often in the lower leg, thigh, or pelvis. When a clot develops, it can partially or completely block normal blood flow, causing pain and swelling in the affected limb.

DVT is a serious condition because a portion of the clot can break loose and travel to the lungs, causing a pulmonary embolism (PE). A PE is a medical emergency and can be life-threatening if not treated promptly.

Early diagnosis and treatment of DVT are critical to protect circulation, reduce symptoms, and help prevent long-term complications such as chronic swelling, pain, or skin changes known as post-thrombotic syndrome.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Not every DVT causes noticeable symptoms, but many patients experience changes in the affected leg. Common features can include:

  • Swelling in one leg (or arm), often developing over hours to days
  • Pain, tenderness, or cramping that may worsen when standing or walking
  • Warmth, redness, or discoloration of the skin over the affected area
  • Veins that appear more prominent or feel firm and sensitive to touch

Symptoms of a pulmonary embolism (PE) may include sudden shortness of breath, chest pain that worsens with deep breathing, rapid heartbeat, coughing up blood, lightheadedness, or fainting. These symptoms are an emergency and require immediate medical attention.

Anyone who suspects a DVT or PE should seek urgent evaluation from a healthcare professional. Diagnosis typically involves imaging studies and blood tests, followed by medication and close follow-up.

Compression Therapy and DVT

Compression therapy is often used to help manage swelling, support venous circulation, and reduce symptoms after a DVT has been diagnosed and treated. In the early, acute phase, decisions about compression are made by the treating physician or vascular specialist based on the individual patient’s condition.

Once medically cleared, graduated compression stockings or pneumatic compression devices may be used to help control swelling, improve comfort, and lower the risk of post-thrombotic syndrome. Proper fit and consistent use are important for obtaining the best results.

Great Lakes collaborates closely with referring providers to ensure that any compression therapy is safe, appropriate, and tailored to each patient’s needs. Compression devices should never be started, stopped, or adjusted without guidance from the healthcare team managing the DVT. Patients should promptly report new or worsening pain, swelling, or changes in skin color to their provider.
