Pneumatic Compression for Arterial Disease

In selected cases of peripheral arterial disease (PAD), pneumatic compression may be used as part of a comprehensive plan to improve circulation, support wound healing, and reduce symptoms such as pain with walking. These devices must always be used under the guidance of a vascular specialist or treating provider.

Before compression is considered, your arterial blood flow is evaluated through clinical examination and vascular testing. This ensures that any compression therapy is safe, appropriate, and consistent with your overall treatment goals.

How the Device Works

For appropriate patients, an arterial-focused pneumatic compression device delivers intermittent pressure at the foot, calf, or leg. This can help promote blood flow through the arteries, support tissue oxygenation, and complement other treatments prescribed by the vascular team.

Pressure settings, inflation patterns, and treatment duration are determined by your provider. During setup, a Great Lakes representative will review your prescribed protocol, demonstrate safe use, and answer questions about comfort and positioning.

These devices are intended to work alongside, not replace, other medical therapies such as risk factor management, medications, or revascularization procedures when indicated.

Who May Benefit

Pneumatic compression for arterial disease may be considered for patients who:

  • Have documented peripheral arterial disease (PAD)
  • Are under the active care of a vascular specialist or provider
  • Have non-healing wounds or rest pain where compression is prescribed
  • Have been specifically evaluated and cleared for this type of therapy

Compression devices should never be used on a limb with critical ischemia or severely reduced arterial blood flow unless explicitly ordered by the treating provider. Any new pain, color change, or coolness should be reported immediately to the care team.
