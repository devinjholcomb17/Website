What Is Arterial Disease?

Arterial disease, often referred to as peripheral arterial disease (PAD), occurs when the arteries that carry blood away from the heart become narrowed or blocked by plaque buildup. This reduced blood flow most commonly affects the legs and feet, but can also involve the arms and other areas of the body.

When tissues do not receive enough oxygen-rich blood, patients may experience pain with walking, cramping, numbness, slow-healing wounds, or skin changes. Left untreated, significant arterial disease can lead to non-healing ulcers, infection, and in severe cases, tissue loss.

Early evaluation and ongoing management are essential to protect circulation, reduce symptoms, and help prevent complications. Great Lakes works closely with referring providers to support safe, effective care for patients living with arterial disease.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms can vary from mild to severe, and some people may not notice any problems early on. Common features of peripheral arterial disease can include:

  • Pain, cramping, or tiredness in the calves, thighs, or buttocks when walking
  • Numbness, weakness, or heaviness in the legs or feet
  • Cool, pale, or discolored skin on the lower legs or toes
  • Wounds or ulcers on the feet or ankles that heal slowly or not at all
  • Decreased hair growth or thickened nails on the legs and feet

Anyone experiencing these symptoms should speak with a healthcare professional for a full evaluation. Prompt diagnosis allows providers to create a treatment plan that may include lifestyle changes, medications, or procedures to improve blood flow.

Compression Therapy and Arterial Disease

Compression therapy is commonly used to support venous and lymphatic circulation. In patients with arterial disease, however, compression must be used carefully and only under the guidance of a medical provider. The level of arterial blood flow must be evaluated before any compression garments or devices are prescribed.

When appropriate and medically cleared, compression can be part of a broader plan to manage swelling, support wound healing, and improve comfort.
